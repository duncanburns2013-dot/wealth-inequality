01 — The Printing Press

Money Printing: $13 Trillion Created from Thin Air

Between 2008 and 2022, the Federal Reserve expanded its balance sheet from $900B to $8.97T and the M2 money supply from $8.5T to $21.7T. This money overwhelmingly flowed into financial assets — stocks, bonds, real estate — not into workers' paychecks.

The Mechanism

The Fed buys Treasury bonds and mortgage-backed securities from banks, crediting their reserves with newly created money. Banks don't lend this to Main Street — they invest it in financial markets, inflating asset prices. Since the top 1% own 54% of all corporate equities, they capture the vast majority of the gains. The bottom 50% — who own just 1.3% of equities — get rising grocery bills instead.

02 — Free Money for Wall Street

ZIRP: 14 Years of Zero Interest Rates

From December 2008 to March 2022, the Federal Reserve held interest rates at or near zero. This destroyed returns for savers (retirees, working families) while making borrowing virtually free for corporations and the wealthy — who used cheap debt to buy back stock, inflate asset prices, and lever up.

Who Won & Who Lost

Winners: Corporations borrowed at near-0% to buy back $7T+ in stock (2009–2022), artificially boosting share prices and executive compensation. Private equity used cheap debt for leveraged buyouts. Real estate investors leveraged into rental properties.
Losers: Savers earned 0.01% on savings accounts while inflation ran 2–9%. Retirees on fixed income saw purchasing power erode. First-time homebuyers were priced out as investors flooded housing markets.

03 — Spending Without Consequence

Government Debt: From $5.6T to $36.2T

Federal debt has grown from 55% of GDP in 2000 to 121% in 2025. Annual deficits routinely exceed $1T even in strong economies. The interest on this debt — now over $1T/year — crowds out spending on infrastructure, education, and direct transfers that would help the bottom 50%.

Who Pays for This?

Deficits are financed by issuing Treasury bonds — bought primarily by the wealthy, foreign governments, and the Fed itself (monetizing the debt). The interest payments ($1T+/year) flow disproportionately to bondholders, while the inflationary effects of deficit spending erode the purchasing power of wages. Working families pay for it through hidden inflation; bondholders profit from it.

04 — The Acceleration Event

COVID Response: $5 Trillion in 18 Months

The pandemic response combined the most aggressive monetary and fiscal expansion in history. The Fed's balance sheet doubled. M2 grew 40% in two years. Congress authorized ~$5.2T in direct spending. The result: the fastest wealth transfer in American history.

Where the Money Actually Went

While stimulus checks sent $800–$3,200 to individuals, the real money went to asset markets. The Fed bought $120B/month in securities. The S&P 500 doubled from March 2020 to December 2021. Home prices surged 40%. The top 1%'s net worth grew by $12 trillion in just two years — more than the entire net worth of the bottom 50%.

07 — Connecting the Dots

The Full Picture

Every policy choice — QE, ZIRP, deficit spending, COVID response — followed the same pattern: channel money through financial markets, inflate asset prices, and let it "trickle down." The data proves it never did.

The Transmission Mechanism — Step by Step

1. The Fed creates money → buys bonds from banks → banks invest in financial assets
2. Asset prices rise → stocks +213%, real estate +60% since 2007 → top 1% (who own 54% of equities) capture majority of gains
3. ZIRP kills saving → 0% on savings accounts → retirees and workers lose purchasing power → forced into riskier assets
4. Corporations lever up → borrow at 0% → buy back $7T+ in stock → executive comp soars → workers get nothing
5. Government deficit spends → $36T debt → $1T/year in interest → paid to bondholders (wealthy, foreign govts)
6. Inflation hits → food, rent, healthcare up 20-40% → erodes real wages → bottom 50% loses purchasing power
7. Labor share declines → 8.9pp drop = ~$2.6T/year shifted from workers to capital
8. Result: Top 1% wealth +213% vs median household income +15% since 2007
